What is Disk Scheduling?

Disk scheduling is how a computer chooses the best order to read or write data on a storage disk. It's like planning a delivery route — it saves time by avoiding back-and-forth movements.

Why is Disk Scheduling Important?

  • Faster Performance: Reduces movement time of the read/write head.
  • Reduces Waiting Time: Efficiently handles multiple requests.
  • Improves System Efficiency: Keeps the system smooth and responsive.

Where is it Used?

Disk scheduling is found in most operating systems and used when multiple tasks need to access storage. It plays a major role in:

  • Operating Systems (Windows, Linux, macOS)
  • Servers and Data Centers
  • Databases and Storage Engines
  • Cloud Storage Systems
  • Smart Devices and Consoles

How Does It Work? (Simple Analogy)

Imagine you're delivering mail to rooms 3, 7, 2, and 9. Instead of jumping randomly between rooms, you go in order — 2 → 3 → 7 → 9 — saving time. Disk scheduling works in a similar way to avoid unnecessary delays.

Common Disk Scheduling Algorithms

  • FCFS: First come, first served — simple and fair.
  • SSTF: Chooses the nearest request to minimize seek time.
  • SCAN: Works like an elevator — moves in one direction serving requests.
  • LOOK: Like SCAN but stops where the requests end.

To sum it up — disk scheduling is like an intelligent traffic controller inside your computer, ensuring that data flows quickly, fairly, and efficiently.

Disk Scheduling Algorithms

FCFS (First Come First Serve)

Imagine you are standing in a queue at a grocery store. The cashier serves the customers one by one in the order they arrived. The first person who comes to the queue gets served first, the second person goes next, and so on.

Imagine you are standing in a queue at a grocery store. The cashier serves the customers one by one in the order they arrived. The first person who comes to the queue gets served first, the second person goes next, and so on.

This is exactly how the First Come, First Serve (FCFS) scheduling algorithm works in an operating system.

How it Works:

  • Processes (or tasks) arrive and are lined up in the order they come.
  • The processor picks the first process in the queue and executes it completely.
  • Once it's done, the next process in line gets executed, and this continues until all processes are finished.

Key Points:

  • Non-preemptive: Once a process starts, it runs until it finishes (no interruptions).
  • Simple and fair: Whoever comes first gets served first.
  • Can cause waiting time issues: If a long process comes first, the smaller ones behind it have to wait (called the convoy effect).

Example:

Process Arrival Time Burst Time
P1 0 ms 5 ms
P2 1 ms 3 ms
P3 2 ms 8 ms

Execution Order: P1 → P2 → P3 (since P1 arrived first, it gets executed first and then other processes).

SSTF (Shortest Seek Time First)

Imagine you are standing in the middle of a long hallway with multiple rooms on both sides. Your job is to deliver packages to these rooms, but you can choose the order in which you deliver them. Naturally, you would deliver to the closest room first to minimize walking distance, right?

In SSTF, the disk head moves to the closest track in the queue, minimizing seek time. This improves overall system performance but can lead to starvation of some requests.

How it Works:

  • The algorithm looks at all pending requests in the queue.
  • It selects the request closest to the current head position.
  • After servicing that request, it again looks for the next closest request.

Key Points:

  • Better Performance: Reduces average seek time compared to FCFS.
  • Potential Starvation: Requests far from the head might wait indefinitely.
  • Dynamic Selection: Next request is chosen based on proximity, not arrival time.

Example:

Let’s say the disk head is at position 50, and we have requests at these positions:

Requests: 20, 45, 65, 10, 90

Execution Order (SSTF chooses the closest first):

  1. Start at 50
  2. Move to 45 (nearest)
  3. Move to 65 (next nearest)
  4. Move to 90
  5. Move to 20
  6. Move to 10

Instead of blindly serving in the arrival order, SSTF intelligently reduces movement to speed things up!

SCAN (Elevator Algorithm)

Imagine you are standing in a long hallway with multiple rooms on both sides. Your job is to deliver packages to these rooms, but you can only move in one direction at a time. You start at one end, deliver packages to the rooms, and then reverse direction to deliver the remaining packages.

The SCAN algorithm, also known as the elevator algorithm, moves the disk head back and forth across the disk surface, servicing requests in each direction.

How it Works:

  • Starts at one end of the disk and moves toward the other end.
  • Services all requests in its path while moving in one direction.
  • When it reaches the other end, it reverses direction and repeats.

Key Points:

  • Bidirectional Movement: Moves back and forth across the disk.
  • Fair Distribution: Prevents starvation by serving requests in both directions.
  • Predictable Pattern: Like an elevator, it follows a consistent pattern of movement.

Example:

Let’s say the disk head is at position 50, and we have requests at these positions:

Requests: 20, 45, 65, 10, 90

Execution Order (SCAN moves in one direction, then reverses):

  1. Start at 50
  2. Move to 65 (nearest in the same direction)
  3. Move to 90 (edge of movement)
  4. Reverse direction
  5. Move to 45
  6. Move to 20
  7. Move to 10

SCAN works like an elevator, moving in one direction until the last request, then reversing to serve remaining requests in the opposite direction!

C-SCAN (Circular Elevator Algorithm)

Imagine you are a bus driver following a fixed route. You start picking up passengers from one end of the city and move towards the other end, stopping at all bus stops along the way. Once you reach the last stop, instead of turning back and picking up passengers on the way back, you go straight to the first stop and start over.

C-SCAN (Circular SCAN) is a variant of the SCAN algorithm that provides more uniform waiting time by treating the disk as a circular list.

How it Works:

  • Moves from one end to the other, servicing requests along the way.
  • When reaching the end, jumps back to the beginning.
  • Only services requests in one direction (usually toward larger track numbers).

Key Points:

  • Uniform Waiting Time: Provides more consistent service than SCAN.
  • One-Way Service: Only services requests while moving in one direction.
  • Quick Return: Fast return to start without servicing requests.

Example:

Let’s say the disk head is at 50, and requests are at these positions:

Requests: 20, 35, 75, 85, 95

Execution Order (C-SCAN moves one way, resets, and continues):

  1. Start at 50
  2. Move to 75
  3. Move to 85
  4. Move to 95 (last request in this direction)
  5. Jump back to the lowest request (20)
  6. Move to 35

Instead of going back and forth like an elevator (SCAN), C-SCAN always moves in one direction and jumps back to start again.
